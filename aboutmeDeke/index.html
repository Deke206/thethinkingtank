The straight answer

Yes, I am living and working out of the van.

You may have driven past and seen me sitting beside it, reaching into it, working on a computer, repairing something, charging equipment or trying to keep a project moving. The van is currently my home, office, storage space, repair bench and transportation.

This is not the life I planned, and it is not proof that I stopped trying. It is what I am working from while I rebuild. The LED banner is not just decoration. It is my way of turning street visibility into a chance: a chance to explain myself, find paid work, connect with somebody who needs what I know how to do, or receive a little help while I build something more stable.

My story

I have been a business owner, a driver, a problem-solver and the person who figures things out.

My name is Dedric “Deke” Alexander. Before this chapter, I owned and operated a limo and town-car business. Running that kind of business meant doing far more than driving. I had to handle customers, scheduling, sales, presentation, marketing, paperwork, vehicle issues and the thousand small things that decide whether a customer calls again. I learned to create materials in Adobe Photoshop and Illustrator because I needed them. I learned how to promote services because I needed customers. I learned how to troubleshoot vehicles, electronics and equipment because waiting for somebody else was not always an option.

I also had periods of more stable work connected to property management and Airbnb operations. During the pandemic I helped an older man with his automotive business. I have experience dealing with people, property, vehicles, online listings, customer needs and practical problems. The pandemic helped knock down the stability I had built, and the years after it did not provide an easy road back.

A lot of public rhetoric makes it easy to look at an unhoused person and assume that person is lazy, dangerous, addicted or waiting for somebody else to fix everything. That is not my story. I am still trying to work. I am still learning. I am still building websites, researching products, diagnosing equipment, testing ideas and looking for ways to turn what I know into dependable income.

I am not presenting myself as perfect. I am presenting myself honestly. I am a capable person in a hard situation, trying to use every tool and skill I still have to change it.

What you are seeing

The van is called Ms. Beastmode.

She is a damaged 1991 Dodge Ram B250 Sportsman. An accident left the van badly damaged, and the other driver left the scene. There was no useful insurance recovery to put things back together.

That matters because this one vehicle has been carrying nearly every part of my life. Damage to it is not just a body-shop problem. It affects where I sleep, how I work, how I store tools, how I move equipment and whether I can get to an opportunity.

I am also dealing with serious health and physical problems. I keep going, but those issues make every setback heavier and every hot day longer.

Why I am trying to leave gig work

Uber helped me survive, but it cannot be my future.

For almost five years, I used Uber and delivery work to keep some money moving. I appreciate the customers who tipped, the restaurants that treated drivers like people and the fact that the app gave me a way to earn when other doors were closed. But the economics became too unstable. There have been times when the income fell to around a hundred dollars a week before counting repairs, batteries, transportation, food, phone service and the physical wear of doing the work.

The platform controls access to the work. New terms and agreements can appear between a driver and the ability to log in. From my perspective, too much risk is pushed toward the person doing the driving while that person has very little control over pricing, access or what happens after an accident. I have also spent time trying to understand California earnings adjustments and whether the records clearly matched the time and miles I put in. That is not where I want to spend the rest of my energy.

I am not trying to get away from work. I am trying to get away from work that can disappear from a screen while leaving me with the expenses and the risk. My goal is to earn directly from useful services, build relationships with real customers and create something that does not depend on an app deciding whether I am allowed to work that day.

What I am building now

I am trying to turn scattered ability into stable, independent income.

ShyneTyme Works is the LED, electronics and creative-installation side. The goal is to design practical programmable lighting for bikes, vehicles, boats, signs and custom projects—not just sell random strips, but help people plan something that works.

Westside Listing Services is the sales, research and sourcing side. The goal is to help people who have something valuable but do not know how to present it, price it, identify it or find the right buyer. It also includes finding hard-to-source parts and specialty items for customers who would rather pay for good research than waste days searching.

I am also open to straightforward paid work right now: website repairs, listing projects, product research, electronics troubleshooting, vehicle-related research, Adobe work and small-business problem solving. A small real job is more valuable to me than a big promise.
